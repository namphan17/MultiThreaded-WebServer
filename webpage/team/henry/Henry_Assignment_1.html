10 Sample Questions About The Material With Answers

Chapter 1 Questions

  1. Q: What are the different types of delay at a node in a packet-switch network?

    A: A node in a packet-switch network may suffer from processing, queuing, transmission, and propogation delays together make up what is called total nodal delay.

  2. Q: What is meant by the phrase "A Network of Networks"?

    A: This is part of our theme of layers of abstraction. There are routers and communication links which span the entire globe, and this in itself is a network. However, there is not one single ISP which manages this entire global network. Instead, ISPs charge and segment networks on an individual basis, or sometimes in small chunks of routers. These are each networks as well, and we therefore call this system a network of interconnected networks.

  3. Q: In our context, what is a protocol?

    A: A protocol defines the format and the order of messages exchanged between two or more communicating entities, as well as the actions taken on the transmission and/or receipt of a message or other event.

Chapter 2 Questions

  1. Q: Please name the two network application architectures and give one advantage of each.

    A: The two architectures are client-server and peer to peer or P2P. Client-server models have many advantages, the primary one being a simple structure which allows end users to access the entire network by connecting solely to the central server, providing ease of access as well as relative security. Peer to peer has the advantage of a direct connection to other users, so that the network connections are not dependent on a single point of failure.

  2. Q: For what kind of application is it critical to have low delay, and for what kind is it not ciritical for low delays, but desirable for high throughput?

    A: Internet telephony, video calling, and gaming requires low delay because the real-time information is critical. For file transfering it is not critical to have low delays, but it is desirable to have high throughput to get the entire file quickly.

  3. Q: What is the relationship between a socket and a port? What are the well known port numbers?

    A: In the context of this course a socket is an application level software component that allows the application to transmit and receive data, and a port is an endpoint on a network which is defined at an operating system level. A socket on an application is designated a port number, allowing the computer to porperly deliver data from the network to the correct process on the local machine.

P26 from Chapter 2. Page 177 Suppose Bob joins a BitTorrent torrent, but he does not want to upload any data to any other peers (so called free-riding).

    a. Bob claims that he can receive a complete copy of the file that is shared by the swarm. Is Bob’s claim possible? Why or why not?

    b. Bob further claims that he can further make his “free-riding” more efficient by using a collection of multiple computers (with distinct IP addresses) in the computer lab in his department. How can he do that?

Answer to part A: Bob's claim is possible. There is nothing inherent in the peer-to-peer architecture to prevent someone from "free-riding". For a time BitTorrent's primary weakness was its reliance on a base of altruistic users. Because of the legal gray area that file sharing services represent, some users are reluctant to expose themselves by sharing parts of files themselves and instead stick strictly to downloading. As recently as five years ago, BitTorrent has been implementing reward systems for users who have a good record of contributing to the community, punishing those who take advantage of the services it has to offer.

Answer to part B: Bob could make his file harvesting more efficient by using multiple computers. Consider the answer to part A which demonstrates that BitTorrent uses a reward system to prevent users from downloading files without contributing themselves. There is nothing to stop Bob from sharing the same piece of a file from multiple different computers, wile using those distinct computers to download distinct portions of a file. This would allow Bob to gain more file fragments per shared fragment, proportional to the number of computers Bob was able to use.

P26 from Chapter 3. Page 293Consider transferring an enormous file of L bytes from Host A to Host B. Assume an MSS of 536 bytes.

    a. What is the maximum value of L such that TCP sequence numbers are not exhausted? Recall that the TCP sequence number field has 4 bytes.

    b. For the L you obtain in (a), find how long it takes to transmit the file. Assume that a total of 66 bytes of transport, network, and data-link header are added to each segment before the resulting packet is sent out over a 155 Mbps link. Ignore flow control and congestion control so A can pump out the segments back to back and continuously.

Answer to Part A: If the Maximum Segment Size is 536 bytes, and the TCP sequence flag requires 4 bytes, then the maximum amount of data in each TCP segment is 536 - 4 = 532 bytes. Since the sequence number is alloted 4 bytes, there can be 2 ^ 32 or 4,294,967,295 distinct segments in the file. This means that the maximum size of L is (2 ^ 32) x 532 bytes = 2.284 terabytes.

Answer to Part B: On a 155 Mbps network, ignoring flow and congestion control, this download should take 2,464.1 hours. Around 100 days.
